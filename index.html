02

Fuel & snacks

Eat to feel light and energized, not stuffed. Stick to foods we already know agree with us — tournament morning is no time to try something new.

  • 🌙The night before. A carb-friendly dinner — pasta, rice, potatoes — tops up our energy stores. Drink plenty of water through the evening, and keep it familiar and not too heavy.
  • 🍳Breakfast, ~1.5–2 hrs before. Something light and carby: oatmeal, a banana, toast with honey or peanut butter, yogurt. Add a little protein (an egg) if there's time. Eat early enough to digest before we play.
  • 🍌Between matches. Quick, easy energy in small bites — banana, dates, pretzels, trail mix, a granola or energy bar, half a sandwich. Top up the tank, don't fill it.
  • 💧Hydration is fuel too. Sip water before we feel thirsty, and add electrolytes for the sun and the back-to-back matches. Start hydrating tonight, not at 7:55 AM.
  • ⚠️Go easy on. Greasy, very sugary, or high-fiber foods right before playing — they sit heavy or cause a spike-and-crash. Save the big celebration meal for after.
  • 🥳Pack a treat for after. Something we'll look forward to. Win or lose, we'll have earned it.
03

What the morning usually looks like

Beginner doubles is friendly and low-pressure — no big crowd and no professional umpires, just a few teams sharing courts with organizers from the committee on hand to keep things running. Here's the typical flow so nothing catches us off guard:

  • iArrive early — 20 to 30 minutes. Check in at the desk, find which court we're on, and leave time to warm up and shake off nerves.
  • iShort matches, played back-to-back. Beginner events are usually a round-robin (everyone plays a few quick matches) or a small bracket — often short sets, first-to-X games, or timed. We may play more than one match with breaks between, so pace our energy and snacks.
  • iQuick warm-up before each match. A few minutes of gentle rallying, some volleys at the net, and a couple of practice serves. Keep warm-up balls soft and friendly.
  • iWe keep our own score. There's usually someone from the committee keeping an eye on things — not a professional umpire — so it's still on us to track the score and call our own lines. Announce the score out loud before every serve so everyone agrees.
  • iSwitch ends on odd games. Change sides after games 1, 3, 5… Grab a sip of water at the changeover.
  • iHandshake at the end. Win or lose, meet at the net, shake hands, "good game." That's the whole ritual.

Warm-up routine

Ten loose minutes wakes up the body, settles the nerves, and prevents pulls. Work down the list once we're on court.

05

Play as one team, not two players

  • ✓Talk every single point. Quick calls: "mine," "yours," "switch," "back." It feels like over-talking — it isn't. Silence is where doubles falls apart.
  • ✓Decide the middle now. Balls down the center go to whoever has their forehand there. Agree before the match so we're never both reaching or both watching.
  • ✓Move together. If one of us steps up, the other steps up too. Imagine a rope tying us — we shift side to side as a pair, never split far apart.
  • ✓Celebrate every point — even lost ones. Fist bump, "next one," reset. No apologizing, no blaming. Confidence is contagious and so is frustration.
06

How beginners actually win

  • ✓Get the ball back one more time than they do. Most points at this level end on errors, not winners. Steady beats flashy. Just keep it in.
  • ✓Get the first serve in. Slow it down if needed — a soft serve in play beats a big one in the net. Double faults are free points handed away.
  • ✓Aim crosscourt and deep. The net is lowest in the middle and the court is longest diagonally — more margin, fewer mistakes.
  • ✓Return down the middle. Splitting the opponents causes confusion (the same trap we're avoiding) and keeps the net player from poaching.
  • ✓Aim at their feet. A ball at the feet is the hardest to volley. Forget the lines — target the gap and the shoelaces.
07

Beating the 8 AM sun

  • ☀Cap or visor + sunglasses. A low morning sun sits right at eye level. A brim plus polarized lenses is the single biggest help — bring them.
  • ☀Sunscreen now, even early. Morning sun still burns over a long match. Reapply at the changeover.
  • ☀Sun in your eyes? Don't fight the overhead. On a high ball or lob into the sun, shade with your free hand, or just let it bounce and play it off the ground. A safe groundstroke beats a blind smash into the net.
  • ☀Tweak your serve toss. If the toss goes straight into the sun, shift it slightly sideways so you're not staring into the glare at contact.
  • ☀The shaded partner takes the lobs. Whoever has the sun behind them should call the high balls and overheads. Say it out loud: "I've got it."
  • ☀It's fair — we switch ends. You change sides on odd games, so the bad end is shared. Note which end blinds you and plan the tougher shots for the friendly end.
08

Do's & don'ts on court

✓ Do

  • + Introduce ourselves and shake hands before we start.
  • + Call the score out loud before each serve.
  • + Give the benefit of the doubt on close line calls — if unsure, it's their point.
  • + Wait for a point to finish before crossing behind another court.
  • + Hold up and return stray balls to the right court between points.
  • + Keep encouraging each other, especially after mistakes.
  • + Hydrate and grab a bite between matches.

✕ Don't

  • – Argue line calls on the opponents' side — their call stands.
  • – Get down on yourself or your partner out loud.
  • – Blast warm-up balls — keep them soft and rally-able.
  • – Go for hero winners on every ball; consistency wins here.
  • – Keep playing if the score's unclear — pause and agree first.
  • – Walk onto or behind a court mid-point.
  • – Show up late and skip the warm-up.
